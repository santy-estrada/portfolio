Pinion Double-Rack System

Modeling, instrumentation, and hybrid control of a mechanical-electrical system

Project Details

CIIMA 2025 (Published) - IEEE CCAC 2025 (Submitted)

This project focuses on the design, construction, and analytical modeling of a double-rack mechanism for advanced system identification and hybrid dynamics research. The initial phase of the project was presented at the XIV Congreso Internacional de Ingeniería Mecatrónica y Automatización - CIIMA 2025. In that study, we implemented a physical prototype consisting of a DC motor, dual opposing racks, and elastic springs, instrumented with current, displacement, and angular velocity sensors.

The research aimed to experimentally identify uncertain dynamic parameters such as armature resistance, inductance, and viscous friction of the DC motor. Data was gathered from the physical system using an Arduino-controlled acquisition setup, and analyzed using MATLAB/Simulink. The resulting dynamic model was refined through grey- and black-box identification methods, showing strong agreement between simulation and experimental results.

Building on this foundation, an extension of the work was submitted to the 7th IEEE Colombian Conference on Automatic Control (CCAC 2025). This second paper introduced a formal hybrid dynamical system (HDS) perspective, modeling the switching behavior inherent in the pinion double-rack setup. The system's behavior was described using hybrid automata, capturing transitions between discrete movement phases based on the spring deformation and angular displacement of the gear.

The hybrid model used a state-space formulation that incorporated the previously identified parameters. Transitions between states were triggered by guard conditions linked to the deformation limits of each spring. Each mode corresponded to either upward or downward motion of one of the racks. The hybrid simulation framework, implemented in Simulink with switching logic, enabled evaluation of the system under realistic constraints, including friction and power consumption.

A key contribution of this work was the analysis of energy consumption under hybrid control. By integrating the absolute value of current over time, we compared the simulation against real measurements and observed a 30.8% increase in experimental energy use, highlighting the gap introduced by electrical and mechanical inefficiencies. Additionally, the system's behavior in phase space confirmed the effectiveness of the hybrid representation in predicting switching dynamics.
