Electric Grid Fault Identification

Unsupervised clustering and signal-modeling for early anomaly detection in CELSIA's distribution network

Project Details

Completed: December 2025

This project explores fault detection in electric distribution grids using only daily energy consumption data (kWh) provided by CELSIA S.A. E.S.P.. Due to the lack of electrical variables such as voltage, current, or power quality metrics, the challenge centered on extracting meaningful anomaly indicators from a low-resolution dataset. The work followed the ASUM-DM data-mining methodology and evaluated several unsupervised models to cluster consumption profiles and detect irregular behavior.

My main contributions included:

  • Full preprocessing pipeline for filtering, cleaning, validating, and transforming all meter data
  • Feature engineering (sinusoidal time encoding, net consumption, contextual variables)
  • Implementation and tuning of multiple clustering algorithms (K-Means, MiniBatch, Birch, DBSCAN, GMM)
  • Hyperparameter optimization using elbow criterion, silhouette analysis, dendrograms, BIC, and density analysis
  • Interpretation of clustering results, with detailed anomaly/outlier characterization
  • Final model selection based on operational constraints and failure-detection goals

Data Understanding & Preprocessing

The original dataset contained daily cumulative energy readings from 401 meters over one year. Because KWH_IMPORT is incremental, all net consumptions were computed using adjacent differences, while discarding invalid or saturated readings. Eight meters with constant consumption were removed.

Time variables were decomposed into sin-cos cyclic encodings (day and month), and meter-level context features were engineered:

  • CONSUMO_NETO - net daily consumption
  • METER_STD_CONSUMO - meter variability
  • CONSUMO_DEVIATION - deviation from the meter's mean behavior
  • Cyclic DIA_sin/cos and MES_sin/cos

After univariate and multivariate exploration, redundant variables were removed and all features were scaled with MinMaxScaler. The final dataset contained 140,628 entries.

Model Construction

Following ASUM-DM guidelines, several clustering algorithms were trained and compared:

  • K-Means: evaluated k = 2-15 using elbow & silhouette analysis
  • MiniBatchKMeans: reduced computational cost, k = 4
  • Birch: hyperparameters tuned to enforce 4 clusters
  • DBSCAN: epsilon and min_samples tuned through k-distance curves and grid search
  • Gaussian Mixture Models: covariance types tested, final model chosen using BIC minimization

Silhouette scores, cluster separability, and operational interpretability were used as the main selection criteria. Although K-Means with k=12 scored highest mathematically, operational constraints favored a reduced number of clusters (≤ 5) to meaningfully classify states such as normal, sector failure, or anomalies.

Results & Cluster Interpretation

The final selected model was K-Means with k = 5, which balanced statistical performance and operational interpretability.

Across all models, the data exhibited high internal variability and large numbers of outliers in every cluster. Boxplots showed that consumption anomalies were not localized to specific groups— instead, outliers appeared throughout the entire network, making it impossible to identify reliable “representative meters” for each cluster.

Cluster means were nearly identical (1.95–2.09 kWh), but their variances differed:

  • Cluster 1 showed the highest variability → noisy or unstable consumption
  • Cluster 0, 2, and 3 were structurally similar
  • Cluster 4 had slightly higher variability but remained within typical ranges

These results indicate that the data naturally separates consumers into behavioral profiles, but does not isolate distinct electrical fault modes. This is consistent with the fundamental limitation: consumption in kWh is an aggregated, low-resolution signal that hides fast transient behaviors typical of real faults.

Conclusions

The project successfully produced a full data-driven characterization of consumption behavior for 401 meters, revealing distinct but subtly separated consumer profiles. However, due to the absence of electrical variables (voltage, current, RMS values), none of the unsupervised models could isolate clear failure states.

The work concludes that:

  • Daily kWh consumption alone is insufficient for reliable fault detection.
  • The clustering results are valuable for consumer profiling, not diagnosis.
  • High variability and widespread outliers reduce cluster separability.
  • A fault-oriented model requires higher-resolution electrical data.

Despite the limitations, the pipeline offers a solid foundation for integrating future high-resolution electrical measurements and for developing anomaly-detection models that do not rely on balanced clusters.
