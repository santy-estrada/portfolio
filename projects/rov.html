Remotely Operated Vehicle (ROV)

Design, construction, programming, and testing of a fully functional aquatic ROV

Project Details

Completed: October 2025

This project consisted in the design, construction, programming, and testing of a Remotely Operated Aquatic Vehicle (ROV). The goal was to create a modular, and robust underwater robot capable of operating at depth while providing real-time feedback to the operator.

The design followed an iterative engineering approach: each prototype was evaluated, improved, and refined to achieve greater buoyancy control, stability, and reliability. The final ROV incorporated:

  • Two high-thrust DC motors for horizontal propulsion.
  • A ballast tank system with end switches to prevent mechanical overextension.
  • A calibrated pressure sensor used as a depth measurement system.
  • Internal moisture detection for safety against flooding.
  • A deadman-switch-based safety system that automatically stops actuation if the operator releases the control.
  • A custom emergency ballast-purge button capable of overriding the deadman switch to force 100% power to the ballast motor.
  • A multipurpose custom PCB designed to support the ROV, SCARA robot, and differential-drive robots

Mechanical Design

The ROV's structure was modeled in CAD and manufactured using a combination of 3D-printed components and lightweight materials for structural integrity. The enclosure for electronics was designed to be fully watertight, incorporating O-rings, cable glands, and gasket interfaces to ensure protection during submersion. All mechanical elements were developed to allow easy modular replacement and iterative improvement.

Key mechanical elements included:

  1. Ballast System: A carefully designed ballast tank capable of controlled flooding and purging, monitored by end switches to avoid unsafe piston motion.
  2. Propulsion: Two thrust motors arranged to produce forward, reverse, and turning motion with differential control.
  3. Depth Sensing: A pressure sensor calibrated and mapped to a depth estimation model.
  4. Watertight Electronics Compartment: Secured with multiple seals and internal humidity detection.

Electronics and PCB Design

The electronics relied on a custom PCB designed to support both the ROV and other robotics platforms developed throughout the course. The board incorporated motor drivers, power regulation, sensor interfaces, communication modules, and failsafe logic.

The ROV communicated with the surface via a tether, enabling stable control and real-time telemetry through the ROS2 ecosystem.

Software Architecture

The ROV's microcontroller was programmed using object-oriented programming (POO) principles and implemented using micro-ROS. The onboard firmware published and subscribed to ROS2 topics, enabling seamless integration with a workstation running ROS2 at the surface.

A custom ROS2 node was developed for interpreting joystick commands and sending them to the ROV through the tether connection.

  • Joystick control over USB.
  • Deadman-switch logic implemented at the node level.
  • Ballast override function mapped to a dedicated button.
  • Real-time depth feedback and moisture alerts.

Testing and Validation

The ROV underwent pool testing to validate buoyancy control, directional stability, depth sensing accuracy, and safety mechanisms. The ballast system demonstrated reliable operation, and the deadman-switch behavior proved essential for ensuring operational safety during unexpected events.

Moisture detection was tested by simulating internal condensation events, confirming that the system successfully raised warnings to the operator.
